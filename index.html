Why Geospatial Matters

Surveying and geospatial technology support construction, infrastructure, transportation, utilities, mapping, development, and land management. These careers combine fieldwork, problem-solving, technology, and purpose.

Real-World Skills

Students learn practical concepts tied to the work professionals actually perform in the field and office, from measurement and mapping to data-driven decision-making.

Modern Technology

Training reflects the tools shaping the profession, including total stations, GNSS/GPS, digital mapping, and emerging geospatial workflows.

Career Pathways

Learners are introduced to careers that can lead to meaningful employment, advancement, certifications, and long-term professional growth.

A Growing Industry with Urgent Demand

The land surveying profession faces a major workforce challenge as experienced professionals retire faster than new ones enter the pipeline. That challenge creates a powerful opportunity for students, schools, and workforce programs.

The challenge

Many regions are experiencing a shortage of future surveyors and licensed professionals. Firms, agencies, and industry partners need better pipelines to attract and prepare the next generation.

  • An aging workforce is approaching retirement
  • Too few students know this career path exists
  • Schools need scalable, modern instructional solutions
  • Industry needs workforce-ready talent with foundational skills

The opportunity

This is exactly where the Geospatial Workforce Institute fits. We help students discover a meaningful career path while helping schools align education with real workforce needs.

  • High-demand career field
  • Strong long-term relevance in every state
  • Connection to construction, engineering, and technology
  • A practical bridge between classroom learning and industry employment
